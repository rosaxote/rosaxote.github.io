It somewhat appears that humanity is a constantly growing neural network, constantly adding new layers and training against the premise "did we survive and thrive?"

Sometimes it even 'forgets' and destroys evidence of its past, in order to move forward.

I'll illustrate this in a couple ways: first, with a quantitative view of humanity's growth, and second, with a qualitative look at how societies and civilizations have revitalized themselves throughout history (related to the concept of context-rot).

#### Humanity's Growth

There are about 8.3 billion people on Earth today, with about 117 billion estimated to ever have been born. Here's a table of some key points in humanity's development, and the cumulative born then: <1>

Date, Key Development, Population, Cumulative born 
3000 BC, Writing, 14 million, 18 billion
1 AD, City-states, 300 million, 55 billion
1650 AD, Scientific revolution, 500 million, 94 billion
1950 AD, Modern age (transit, communication, medicine, agriculture), 2.5 billion, 108 billion
1970 AD, Computing, 3.7 billion, 111 billion
2026 AD, Artificial intelligence, 8.3 billion, 117 billion

Humanity can be likened to a neural network due to its ability to forward propagate information. In the early days, we did this through language, drawings, and oral traditions. Later, we invented writing, and this allowed us to transmit more information, with more detail, than ever before. Eventually, we invented the printing press, photography, telegraph/radio, videography, computing, the Internet, etc. all of which enabled us to share and propagate copious amounts of information.

It seems that at each stage of growth, new capabilities were unlocked.

#### Size of Models

The size of AI models are often described by number of parameters -- e.g. the number of internal variables generated during training (also called weights). Let's look at the size of key models over time and their capabilities.<2>

Model, Year, Development, Parameters
LeNet-5, 1998, Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for digits, 60,000
AlexNet, 2012, Won ImageNet, 60 million
ResNet, 2015, Surpassed human-level performance on ImageNet, 25-60 million
Bert, 2018, Human-level performance on reading comprehension, 110-340 million
GPT-2, 2019, Zero-shot task performance, 1.5 billion
GPT-3, 2020, Zero and few shot prompting, 175 billion
GPT-4, 2023, Multi-step logical reasoning, multi-modality, undisclosed but est. 1.8 trillion

Now, of course, size isn't everything, and models like Meta's Llama and DeepSeek show you can retain high performance with fewer parameters (via more/better training data, better architecture, etc.). But, on the whole (and perhaps up to a point), increasing size does unlock more capabilities. 

If individual humans are a proxy for parameters -- with their distillation and weighting of information -- you could say that roughly, ~100 billion is the threshold for achieving a population-wide intelligence. That is, at this point you are able to achieve far more than you could at even 50 billion.
