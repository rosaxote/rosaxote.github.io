It somewhat appears that humanity is a constantly growing neural network, constantly adding new layers and training against the premise "did we survive and thrive?"

Sometimes it even 'forgets' and destroys evidence of its past, in order to move forward.

I'll illustrate this in a couple ways: first, with a quantitative view of humanity's growth, and second, with a qualitative look at how societies and civilizations have revitalized themselves throughout history (related to the concept of [context rot-link]).

Humanity's Growth

Artificial intelligence models are often described by their number of parameters -- e.g. the number of internal variables it comes up with and adjusts during training (also called weights). If each person is essentially a parameter -- with their distillation and weighting of information* -- at which number would new capabilities be unlocked? How does this compare with machine learning models?

3000 BC

Humanity is able to approximate a neural network due to its ability to forward propagate information. In the early days, we did this through language, drawings, and oral traditions. Later, around 3000 BC, we invented writing, and this allowed us to transmit more information, with more detail, than ever before. 

At this point, there were about 14 million people on Earth, and about 18 billion who had ever been born.

1 AD

The population explodes after the establishment of literate city-states. The world population is now 300 million, with about 55 billion cumulatively born. However, only a small share of this population are literate and able to pass on their thoughts to future generations. Despite that, the amount of information propagated is far greater than pre-3000 BC. 

1650 AD

After 1 AD, the West declines and loses much of its knowledge, while the Far East, Middle East, and even Americas reach greater heights. The printing press is invented. However, a terrible plague ravages much of the world in the 1300s, causing the population to dip. 

By the dawn of the age of enlightenment in the 1600s, there are about 500 million people on Earth, and about 94 billion who had ever lived.

1950 AD

A lot has happened in the few centuries since our last check-in. The world discovers basic physical laws, experiences the Industrial revolution, invents the steam engine, telegraph/radio, light bulbs, airplanes, modern antibiotics, the transistor, and much more. Crucially, it invents even finer ways to document knowledge, including photography, videography, audio recordings, etc. 

It has also gone through two World Wars, which displaces and moves people all around the world.

Now there are about 2.5 billion people on Earth, and about 108 billion who ever lived.

1970s AD

Growth is just bonkers. The world population is at a peak growth rate, with 3.7 billion people and 111 billion cumulatively. The Internet and personal computing is just around the corner, leading to even more information available for the billions of people to consume and propagate.

Today, 2026 AD

The world population is about 8.3 billion. About 117 billion people have ever been born. We are awash in data and information, so much that we can train artificial neural networks to some of our bidding. Humans have so much information coming at them that it's actually a bit too much. There is also a lot of low-quality, noisy data. 

However, the beauty of the human neural network is that it has a simple training mechanism: what works for advancement gets propagated forward (whether culturally or literally, through who is around to teach the next generation(s)).

The Size of Humanity's Model

The size of artificial intelligence models are often described by their number of parameters -- e.g. the number of internal variables it comes up with and adjusts during training (also called weights). If each person is essentially a parameter -- something that provides input in the overall model's learning, how would the human network compare with modern machine learning models?





WHY HUMANS DIFFER FROM INSECTS/ANIMALS

Well, you might interject, there are certainly a large number of Antarctic krill on this Earth, to the tune of several hundred trillion. Why have they not also learned and evolved our level of capability? 

I think it's because they lack real memory -- while nature has a (albeit slow) version of memory, that of encoding changes in genes, this memory far trails ours, due to key inventions. First, language allowed humans to transfer generational knowledge through oral traditions. Then, writing enabled that transfer with greater accuracy and detail than ever before. Then came the printing press, then photographs and video records, computing memory, the Internet, etc. 

While some things are lost to time, these advancements have given us great access to knowledge accumulated through past generations -- essentially letting the thoughts of someone from the 15th century be part of our model today. Insects and animals have instinct and the memories ingrained in their DNA, yes, but not to the extent that we have collected.
