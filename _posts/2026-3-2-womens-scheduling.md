In computer science, a common problem is that of scheduling -- basically, when you give tasks to your computer, in what order should it complete them to maximize its processing capacity while minimizing time spent and latency?

There are many types of solutions, ranging from very simple (say, first come first serve) to a little more optimal (say, shortest job first), to rather complex (multilevel feedback queue).

You've likely encountered the same problem in your daily work life. Given a set of tasks, how should you best go through them? Should you get the small tasks out of the way first? Or should you tackle the big priority tasks, no matter the time it might take? Perhaps you should schedule the big priority tasks when you have most mental throughput, as well as long uninterrupted time (as a morning bird or night owl, this might be when the world is sleeping). Or perhaps, you will assign each task an allotted time -- say, 25 minutes, as in the Pomodoro system.

In any case, many situations have some sort of solution. These are called tractable scheduling problems. There exists a solution to scheduling the tasks such that you can, more or less, get them done in a reasonable time.

However, add a few variables in the system and you go from tractable and simple to very, very thorny. For example, perhaps there are due dates on certain tasks. Or, maybe a large task is being blocked by a smaller task. Perhaps many of your tasks have prerequisites you must complete before even considering them. Perhaps your task list is a mess of deadlines, priorities, and prerequisites.

Now, quickly, you have stepped into the realm of intractable problems -- the problems for which there is no efficient solution.

### Family and Career

I have given this prelude because there is a real-life example of a thorny scheduling problem playing out in human society right now, and it's impacting the birth rate of nations around the globe. It's the scheduling problem of women, their careers, and reproduction.

As a woman in modern society, there are a number of tasks to achieve: education, work, marriage, childbirth and rearing. There is a certain order expected and prerequisites involved as well: for example, you often have to first get educated, find a partner within your reproductive lifespan (but not too early, tsk tsk), date to ascertain compatibility, receive a marriage proposal, get married, and have kids -- in that sequence (some people are able to get away with doing things "out of order", but for many, societal pressure compels them to follow that order).

All the while, society adds complications such as 1) making it harder to support a family on a single income, such that the woman would likely need to work as well, which means that 2) she needs to find meaningful work and climb the career ladder, all the while 3) trying to find suitable dates amidst constant geographic uprooting for said meaningful work, and those dates being uprooted themselves, often resulting in 4) geographies segregated by gender (e.g. women in East coast cities dominated by media, fashion, policy, etc. while men flock to the Western cities with more engineering, outdoors access, etc.), adding on 5) the rise of technology fueling both disconnection and frivolous interactions (including endless swiping apps) ... and all this before she actually gets married and has to plan a pregnancy around said career. No wonder a record number of women struggle to complete all their tasks!

![wage gap](/images/wage-gap.jpeg)

So what happens? The same thing that happens in the mechanical, passionless innards of a computer: packet loss, procrastination, thrashing, crashing. Some women find that they have to drop one goal in order to pursue another. Others, confronted with their reproductive timeline, thrash from one relationship to the next without giving any one a chance to progress. Others find that they simply have to buck the order and the prerequisites.

If we're going to keep the human population at replacement rate, we're going to have to help women solve this intractable scheduling problem. 

### Things that could help

Here are a few thoughts on things that would help, though this question certainly deserves a deep treatment on its own: 

Extending female fertility. We are making headways in that with IVF, egg freezing, surrogacy, new medical treatments. If women had a similar reproductive timeline as men, that would be a game-changer.

Remote work allowing more flexible geography, and thus mixing. Perhaps someone working in media doesn't have to spend all her time in NYC. Remote work also helps new parents and parents of young kids. Though again, remote work can make it harder for young people to meet each other.

AI. Maybe AI will do our work and give us more time to have kids ... or maybe it will make us all work harder to compete with AI. Who knows. Worth listing anyway.

I was tempted to add in "make things more affordable", but I think that is too blunt of a statement. After all, some low-income families have some of the highest fertility rates. But I think an under-the-radar policy is simply to increase housing stock. More homes = cheaper homes = more space, resources to rear a family. More homes in high-demand cities = people don't have to choose between having a reasonable commute vs. grow a family.

It's an area where more ideas, and better policy is needed!
